I am an avid science communicator and enjoy both teaching and outreach activities. I have co-taught undergraduate classes of ~ 100 students at ETH Zurich, supervised undergraduate and masters theses, given invited pedagogical lectures, organized and delivered a lecture series on special relativity and electrodynamics to students, and given masters-level lectures on special topics in Computational Physics and Quantum Field Theory.

I have contributed to organizational and co-ordination activities, both for scientific conferences and meetings. I co-ordinated the activities of the Cosmology group at ETH Zurich for the ‘Scientifica’ – a three-day, Zurich-wide public event covering all fields of science, jointly organized by the ETH Zurich and the University of Zurich – and attracting about 30000 attendees from Zurich and surrounding areas. The theme of the event was ‘What data can reveal’ and my involvement also included giving a public lecture on the theme of ‘Big Data in the Invisible Radio Universe’ followed by an interaction session with the public.

The Square Kilometre Array (SKA) will be the world's largest radio telescope. As a member of the Swiss SKA Communications and Outreach Network (SKACON), I regularly participate in liaison meetings with the international SKA Communications team, with a view to increasing communication and awareness about the SKA project in Switzerland.

We recently concluded a highly successful mini-campaign to commemorate this launch, with statements from scientists throughout Switzerland to develop awareness on the scientific goals of the SKA. The news releases at the Department of Theoretical Physics are available here: in French and in English.

The Federal Council approved the accession of Switzerland to the SKAO on 17 December 2021.

I am part of the COMAP (CO Mapping Array Project) survey, which aims to detect carbon monoxide (CO) via intensity mapping in the earliest galaxies of the Universe. COMAP is the first purpose-built experiment targeting a line other than the 21 cm, to reach the milestone of early science results. Using data from thirteen months of observing, COMAP placed the first direct 3D constraint on the CO clustering power spectrum at z ~ 3. My research had incorporated, for the first time, the different CO observational constraints in a consistent manner into a theoretical framework, forming the basis of an updated model developed by the COMAP team for the interpretation of their intensity mapping observations, and allowing for upper limits on how much cold gas must be present in galaxies at the epoch of Galaxy Assembly.

The COMAP news releases are available at Caltech and the University of Geneva.
