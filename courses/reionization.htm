Observing the Cosmic Dawn: how did the first stars and galaxies form?

Outline:

The era of 'Cosmic Dawn', representing the formation of the first stars and galaxies in our Universe, is widely considered the "final frontier" of cosmological surveys today. This course is intended to provide a self contained introduction to the processes governing the majority of baryonic material (atoms and molecules) from Cosmic Dawn to the present day, in a style accessible to students of theoretical physics.

In particular, it addresses the formation of the first stars and galaxies, the intergalactic medium, and the Epoch of Reionization - the second major phase transition of almost all the baryons (after recombination). It provides a direct link between theory and the latest experiments, summarizing several open questions to be explored in the near future.

The three courses, Cosmology (14P013CE), Galaxies and Cosmology: an Introduction (14A033) and Galaxies and Cosmology II - Galaxy Evolution in a Cosmological Context (14A051) are linked to the concepts introduced in this course (though these are not pre-requisites).

The scheme of covered topics is:

  • Introduction and cosmological background, the gravitational growth of structure
  • Radiative transfer in an expanding Universe, the intergalactic medium
  • The first stars, ionization, recombination processes, heating and gas temperature
  • The reionization of cosmological baryons
  • Observing the period of reionization : techniques and instruments, new generation of large telescopes
  • Current research and the coming decade: surveys of high-redshift galaxies, helium reionization, intensity mapping and 21 cm cosmology
